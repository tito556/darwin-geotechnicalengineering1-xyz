AS 1726 requires reliable ground models, but in Darwin the lateritic profile makes conventional drilling alone a risky bet. Deep weathering can mask bedrock, and cavities in the porous ferricrete cap create blind spots for standard investigation methods. Seismic tomography cuts through the ambiguity. It maps P-wave and S-wave velocity gradients across the Cullen Sandstone and underlying metasediments, giving engineers a continuous cross-section rather than isolated point data. For projects near the Cox Peninsula or within the Darwin rural area, where refusal depths vary dramatically over short distances, this geophysical approach reduces the number of boreholes needed while improving confidence in the ground model. The result is a defensible seismic site class and a clearer picture of what lies beneath the tropical soils of Darwin.

A continuous velocity cross-section replaces guesswork where lateritic weathering hides the true bedrock surface beneath Darwin.

Technical details of the service in Darwin

Darwin’s monsoonal cycle saturates the upper 2–4 m of soil for half the year, then bakes it dry from May to October. That seasonal swing changes near-surface velocities enough to confuse a poorly timed seismic survey. Our field crew schedules acquisition during the dry season or early in the buildup, when moisture contrast is lowest and the water table is stable. We run 24- or 48-channel spreads with a sledgehammer source for refraction microtremor and MASW-derived Vs profiles, complementing the tomography line. When the brief demands depth to fresh rock for pile socket design in the Darwin CBD, we switch to a weight-drop or accelerated source to penetrate the weathered zone. The processed tomogram is then cross-checked against existing SPT drilling logs or test pits where accessible, tying geophysical boundaries to physical samples. This integrated workflow avoids the classic pitfall of interpreting a refractor as bedrock when it is actually a cemented hardpan layer—a common issue across the greater Darwin region.

Risks and considerations in Darwin

A mid-rise development on McMinn Street hit refusal at 6 m and assumed it had found rock. Core drilling later proved it was a floating ferricrete boulder, with 12 m of compressible clay beneath. Seismic tomography run beforehand would have shown the velocity inversion immediately. False bedrock is the most expensive misidentification in Darwin geotechnics—it leads to under-designed piles and differential settlement that shows up within the first two wet seasons. Low-velocity zones hidden inside a high-velocity cap also signal cavities or deeply weathered dykes that can collapse during excavation. The cost of a 115 m seismic line is trivial compared to redesigning foundations after the piling rig hits refusal where the model says it should not. For critical infrastructure near the waterfront, we also run shear-wave velocity profiling along the same spread to assess site class per AS 1170.4, because a single N-value correlation just does not capture the lateral variability Darwin ground is known for.

Our services

Our Darwin seismic surveys are configured for the ground conditions contractors actually encounter—weathered profiles, variable water tables, and the need for data that holds up under peer review.

Seismic Refraction Tomography

2D P-wave velocity models for rippability assessment, depth to bedrock, and identification of paleochannels or cavities. Shot records processed with iterative inversion to resolve lateral velocity gradients typical of Darwin’s lateritic terrain.

Questions and answers

What is the typical cost for a seismic refraction survey in Darwin?

For a single 115 m spread processed as a 2D tomogram, budget between AU$4,170 and AU$8,760. The final figure depends on access, line length, source type required to reach target depth, and whether MASW is added to the acquisition.

How does seismic tomography handle Darwin’s lateritic hardpan?

The hardpan presents as a high-velocity cap, but the tomographic inversion models the velocity gradient below it. If velocity drops with depth, the software flags a low-velocity zone rather than forcing a continuous refractor. Cross-checking with existing borehole data confirms whether the cap is true bedrock or a cemented layer.

Can you run the survey during the wet season?

It is possible, but data quality suffers. Saturated near-surface layers attenuate high frequencies and reduce resolution. We strongly prefer May through October for Darwin surveys, when the water table is stable and ground coupling is consistent.

What deliverables do I receive for a foundation design project?

You get a 2D P-wave velocity tomogram with interpreted geological boundaries, a rippability chart tied to velocity ranges, a Vs30 estimate if MASW was acquired, and a geophysical report signed by an experienced geophysicist. Data files are supplied in industry-standard SEG-Y and DXF formats.
