The soil profile changes fast when you move from the Cullen Bay sandstone ridges down toward the Ludmilla Creek floodplain. One site sits on shallow rock, the next hits 12 metres of saturated silty clay. In Darwin, relying on a single borehole to connect the dots between these two points is a gamble. We run vertical electrical sounding (VES) to fill the gaps. A resistivity profile gives us a continuous cross-section of the subsurface, flagging transitions between weathered laterite, marine clay and fresh rock before the excavator arrives. When combined with a CPT test or targeted test pits, the interpretation tightens up considerably, which matters on tight urban sites in Parap or Stuart Park where access limits drilling.

In weathered tropical profiles, resistivity contrasts often reveal the base of the lateritic zone better than SPT blow counts do.

Technical details of the service in Darwin

The most common mistake we see on Darwin jobs is assuming a uniform clay layer based on one borehole log. Lateritic profiles here are notoriously variable – a ferricrete cap can pinch out within three metres, leaving soft kaolinitic clay underneath. VES catches these lateral changes because we measure apparent resistivity at expanding electrode spacings. A Schlumberger array with AB/2 distances up to 100 metres lets us resolve layers to roughly 25–30 metres depth, which covers most shallow foundation and excavation problems. On sites near Rapid Creek, where groundwater is tidally influenced, we schedule readings during low tide to minimise the conductive smearing effect of saline porewater on the resistivity curve. The data inversion is run against borehole control where available, and we present results as a geoelectric section with interpreted lithology. For deeper targets or where we suspect voids in the dolomitic units south of the city, we often supplement VES with MASW to cross-check the shear-wave velocity structure.

Risks and considerations in Darwin

On the coastal plain between Nightcliff and East Arm, we frequently encounter saline groundwater that drops bulk resistivity below 5 Ω·m. If the client only wants a single geophysical method, that conductive layer masks deeper resistive targets, and the interpretation becomes ambiguous. Worse, some contractors treat VES as a standalone tool and skip the borehole calibration entirely. Without at least one logged borehole to constrain the inversion, you can get a mathematically perfect fit that is geologically wrong – a thin, highly resistive duricrust can look identical to a thick, moderately resistive sandstone on the modelled curve. In karst-prone zones near the Stuart Highway corridor south of Palmerston, unrecognised voids or pinnacled rockhead create genuine construction risk. We flag these limitations in the report and always recommend a multi-method approach when ground conditions are complex.

Our services

Our electrical resistivity work in the Top End covers three distinct applications, from routine site classification to detailed groundwater mapping.

Depth to rock and rippability

VES profiling for cut-and-fill earthworks. We identify the base of weathered laterite and estimate rippability for scrapers and ripper dozers, reducing the need for probe drilling on large Darwin subdivisions.

Groundwater table mapping

Seasonal monitoring of the freshwater lens and saline interface using repeat VES stations. Useful for dewatering design on basement excavations near the Darwin CBD waterfront.

Sand and gravel resource exploration

Resistivity contrasts between sand, clay and gravel are strong in the Howard River alluvium. We run 2D profiles to estimate overburden thickness and locate commercial sand deposits.

Frequently asked questions

What depth can a VES survey reach in Darwin soils?

With a maximum AB/2 electrode spacing of 100 metres, we typically resolve geoelectric layers down to 25–30 metres in the lateritic profiles common around Darwin. The actual depth of investigation depends on the resistivity contrast between layers and background noise levels; saturated clays reduce penetration compared to dry sands.

What does a VES survey cost for a residential block?

For a single Schlumberger sounding with calibrated inversion and a summary report, expect a range between AU$1020 and AU$1.430 depending on access conditions and whether we need to clear vegetation for electrode layout. Multi-sounding profiles or 2D resistivity lines are quoted per linear metre.

How long does a VES survey take on site?

A single vertical electrical sounding with four electrode expansions takes roughly 45 to 60 minutes on clear ground. We can complete two to three soundings in a standard field day, plus time for GPS positioning and site notes. The final interpreted report is usually delivered within three to five working days.

Does VES work during the wet season when the ground is saturated?

The reference range for this service in Darwin is AU$1020 - AU$1.430. The final price depends on the project scope and volume.

Can electrical resistivity locate underground utilities?

Resistivity methods detect buried metallic pipes and cables as strong conductive anomalies, but VES is not the primary tool for utility locating. For clearance before drilling or excavation, we recommend a dedicated electromagnetic (EM) sweep or ground-penetrating radar survey. If we encounter a utility signature during a sounding, we note it in the report and adjust the electrode spread position.
